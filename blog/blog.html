What are the differences between relative, absolute, and fixed positioning?

Relative Positioning

Relative to Normal Flow:Elements with relative positioning are positioned relative to their normal position in the document's flow. This means they still occupy space in the document flow, and nearby elements are not affected.
Offset Properties: You can use properties liketop,right,bottom, andleft to shift the element from its normal position.
Example:If you set position: relative; top: 20px;, the element will be moved 20 pixels down from its original position within the normal flow.

Absolute Positioning:

Relative to Closest Positioned Ancestor: Elements with absolute positioning are positioned relative to their closest positioned (non-static) ancestor. If no such ancestor exists, they are positioned relative to the initial containing block (usually the viewport).
Taken Out of Normal Flow: Elements with absolute positioning are taken out of the normal document flow, which means they do not affect the layout of nearby elements.
Offset Properties: You can use properties like top,right,bottom, andleftto specify the exact position of the element relative to its ancestor.
Example: If you set position: absolute; top: 0;left : 0;, the element will be positioned at the top-left corner of its closest positioned ancestor.

Fixed Positioning:

Relative to the Viewport: Elements with fixed positioning are positioned relative to the viewport, which means they stay in the same position even when the page is scrolled.
Taken Out of Normal Flow: Like absolute positioning, elements with fixed positioning are taken out of the normal document flow.
Offset Properties: You can use top, right,bottom , and left to specify the exact position of the element relative to the viewport.
Example: If you set position: fixed;top: 0;right: 0;, the element will be positioned at the top-right corner of the viewport and stay there while scrolling.
